## Overview: NIE vs NIF vs TIE

If you plan to live, work, invest, or buy property in Spain, you will hear several acronyms. The **NIE (Número de Identificación de Extranjero)** is the foreigner identification number every non-Spanish national uses for almost any official interaction. The **NIF** is the general tax identification number that Spaniards and foreigners share, and for foreigners, it matches the NIE. Meanwhile, the **TIE (Tarjeta de Identidad de Extranjero)** is the physical residency card issued to non-EU citizens once their stay exceeds 90 days. Think of the NIE as the unique number that unlocks your Spanish life: you need it to sign for utilities, pay taxes, open a bank account, or complete a property purchase.

Unlike the TIE, the NIE is not proof of legal residency. It is purely an identification number that links your identity to Spanish administration systems. EU citizens and non-EU citizens both need it, and you can apply long before relocating. Because the NIE and NIF match, once you receive the NIE, you can register with the tax agency or submit returns without additional steps.

## Who needs an NIE number

Anyone who wants to engage in economic, professional, or social activities in Spain will eventually need an NIE. That includes:

- Buyers or sellers of Spanish property.
- Founders or investors in Spanish companies.
- Employees signing Spanish work contracts.
- Remote workers renting long-term accommodation.
- Students opening bank accounts or signing housing agreements.
- Family members applying for residency permits.

Even short-term visitors often benefit from having an NIE because it simplifies opening utilities, buying a car, or paying property taxes. Children need NIE numbers too when they are party to inheritance, property purchases, or long-term residency processes. The only people who can avoid it are tourists staying in hotels for short trips with no financial ties.

## Where to apply: Spain or abroad

You have two routes: applying while you are physically in Spain or through a Spanish consulate abroad. The best route depends on your timeline, your location, and current appointment availability.

### Applying inside Spain

If you are already in Spain, you submit your application at a **Comisaría de Policía** or **Oficina de Extranjería** (foreigners office). Major cities such as Madrid, Barcelona, and Valencia require an appointment via the *sede electrónica* (online portal). As of April 2026, availability is extremely limited due to a nationwide administrative backlog. Applying in Spain is the fastest way to obtain an NIE when you already have supporting documents, provided you can secure an elusive slot.

### Applying from abroad

When you need the NIE to buy property or sign contracts before moving, apply through your nearest Spanish consulate. Consulates in London, Dublin, New York, Miami, Sydney, and many other cities accept NIE applications by appointment. The benefit is you can organize everything before traveling. The trade-off is that each consulate sets its own requirements, and processing in 2026 often takes over a month because documents must be registered in the central Spanish system.

Whichever path you choose, the underlying documentation and fee remain the same. What changes are the appointment booking method and how you collect the certificate.

## Documents checklist and EX-15 walkthrough

The paperwork looks intimidating but becomes manageable when you break it down. Assemble the following:

1. **Passport and copies.** Bring the original passport plus one or two photocopies of the information page. EU citizens can often use national identity cards.
2. **Completed EX-15 form.** This is the official application form for non-resident NIEs. Fill it in using capital letters, and while English guides exist, the submitted version must be in Spanish.
3. **Justification letter.** Provide evidence of why you need an NIE, such as a property reservation contract, job offer, or company incorporation documents.
4. **Proof of address.** Some offices request a local Spanish address. Temporary accommodation or a hotel booking usually suffices for the form.
5. **Passport-size photos.** While usually only for TIE cards, some local police stations still require two recent photos for NIE records.
6. **Modelo 790 Código 012 fee receipt.** You must pay the administrative fee in advance. More on this below.
7. **Representation authorization (if applicable).** If someone attends on your behalf, they need a notarized power of attorney and a copy of your passport.

### Completing the EX-15 form

The EX-15 has three sections:

- **Section 1** covers personal information: names, date of birth, country of birth, and parent names. Your details must match your passport exactly.
- **Section 2** requests your address and contact information. If you are applying from abroad, enter your home-country address and include a valid email for the digital certificate.
- **Section 3** asks for your reason for applying. Select the most relevant option (economic, professional, social) and briefly describe your situation, for example, "Compra de vivienda en Alicante".

Sign and date the form, ensuring the signature matches your passport. Use blue ink if you are printing and signing manually.

## Paying the Modelo 790 Código 012 fee

Every NIE application requires payment of the **Modelo 790 Código 012** fee. The process is:

1. Visit the official payment website and choose "Asignación de Número de Identidad de Extranjero (NIE)".
2. Complete the form with your personal details. If you do not yet have an NIE, leave that field blank.
3. Download and print the PDF with three copies.
4. Take the forms to a collaborating bank and pay the fee. As of early 2026, the fee is **€12.00**.
5. The bank will stamp the forms and return your copies. Keep one for your records and bring one to the appointment.

If you apply via a consulate, you often pay the fee there in local currency. Check the consulate's specific instructions because some now require payment via bank transfer days before your appointment.

## Booking the appointment

Appointment availability is the biggest hurdle in 2026. Tips for success:

- **Check early on Mondays.** New slots for the following week often appear on Monday mornings around 8:00 AM or 9:00 AM.
- **Select the correct procedure.** Use the "Asignación de NIE" option. If you are an EU citizen seeking residency, choose "Certificados UE".
- **Use the Sede Electrónica.** Have your passport number and name ready to copy-paste. The portal times out quickly.
- **Consider alternative locations.** Applicants in Barcelona often find faster slots in smaller towns within the same province, such as Vic or Manresa.
- **The "Regularization" Impact.** Be aware that the launch of the 2026 regularization program has filled many administrative slots, making slots for simple NIEs harder to find.

Consulates usually provide an appointment booking link. These slots fill up weeks in advance, so schedule as soon as you know you need the NIE.

## What happens on appointment day

Arrive 15 minutes early with your complete physical pack. Digital versions on your phone are generally not accepted at the counter. Inside, follow these steps:

1. Present your passport, EX-15, justification documents, and the stamped fee receipt.
2. Officers may ask simple questions about your reason for applying. Answer succinctly and respectfully.
3. If everything is in order, you receive a stamped copy of the EX-15 or a *resguardo* (receipt) confirming submission.
4. In 2026, most offices no longer issue paper certificates on the spot. You will usually receive the certificate as a PDF via email within 5 to 10 days.

If you gave a power of attorney, your representative signs on your behalf and handles the collection.

## Receiving your NIE certificate

The final document is the **Certificado de Asignación de NIE**, a white sheet listing your number, name, and nationality. Keep digital and physical copies because banks, notaries, and tax authorities will request it frequently. If issued at a consulate, the certificate is sent as a PDF to the email you provided on the EX-15.

Remember that the NIE does not expire, but the physical certificate might be requested by banks to be "recent" (issued within the last three months). If yours is older and rejected, you can request a duplicate using a new fee receipt.

## Troubleshooting common issues

- **No appointments available.** Expand your search, work with an immigration lawyer who has "Red SARA" access, or apply through a consulate abroad.
- **Justification rejected.** Ensure your document is in Spanish or has a sworn translation. For property, a signed *arras* (deposit) contract is the gold standard.
- **Incorrect details.** Review the PDF as soon as it arrives. If there is a typo, you must return to the office to request a correction immediately.
- **Lost NIE certificate.** Since certificates are now primarily digital PDFs, simply reprint your original file. If you only have a paper copy and lose it, you must pay the fee again for a duplicate.
- **Passport validity.** Most offices will reject applications if your passport has less than six months of validity remaining.

## FAQs

### Can someone else collect my NIE for me?

Yes. Provide a notarized power of attorney authorising them to act on your behalf. Since certificates are now sent via email, ensure the representative provides your correct email address.

### Do I need a Spanish phone number to book the appointment?

No, but it is highly recommended. The online booking system sends a verification code via SMS, and while it occasionally works with international numbers, it is most reliable with a Spanish +34 number.

### How soon before a property closing should I apply?

In 2026, you should apply at least three months before completion. The current backlog in cities like Malaga and Valencia means a "last-minute" NIE is almost impossible without professional help.

### Is the NIE the same as a social security number?

No. The social security number is for employment and healthcare. You need the NIE first to apply for a social security number, which is usually issued on the same day by the Tesorería de la Seguridad Social.
